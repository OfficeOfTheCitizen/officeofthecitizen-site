Chapter 1

Purpose and scope

The 100 Archive Project is a public record of civic consequence in Nigeria’s Fourth Republic. It documents how power is exercised, identifies responsibility across the accountability chain, preserves evidence trails, and enables diagnostic analysis of institutions and democratic systems.

The Archive is not a scandal vault. It is not a news site. It is not a political tool. It is a civic record: the permanent, sourced, structured documentation that functional democracies require and that Nigeria’s democratic history has never had in this form.

The RuleAn archive that documents only failures becomes cynicism. This Archive documents failures, contested events, reforms, positive precedents, and institutional conduct. It is a record of democracy in practice: what worked, what failed, who was responsible, and what changed.

The methodology fixes the Archive’s scope on Nigeria first, 1999 to the present, and covers public institutions plus non-state actors where they materially shape public outcomes. It explicitly governs all editorial staff, contributors, partners, researchers, fact-checkers, and interns with editorial responsibility.

  • Federal and state institutions, ministries, departments, and agencies
  • The judiciary at all levels
  • The National Assembly and state legislatures
  • INEC and state electoral bodies
  • Security agencies
  • Contractors, political parties, corporations, and development partners where their conduct materially shapes public outcomes
Chapter 2

The accountability framework

Every case must attempt to answer three accountability questions. Where a question cannot be answered, the gap becomes part of the permanent record. The methodology maps responsibility independently by tier rather than inferring upward or downward without documentary support.

TierQuestionTypical actors
Tier 1Policy Responsibility: who designed the rule, policy, mandate, or decision path?President, governors, ministers, policy-setting regulators
Tier 2Institutional Responsibility: who led the institution that should have prevented or handled it?Directors-general, commission chairpersons, institutional heads
Tier 3Operational Responsibility: who executed the action or omission?Department heads, local officials, contract supervisors, implementation authorities
Tier 4Structural Failure: where individual responsibility cannot be identified because the system made accountability impossibleUsed only with Editorial Board approval

Every tier attribution must be grounded in a Class A or Class B source. Where a contractor, private company, political party, or other non-state actor materially shapes the outcome of a governance failure, they are documented as a contributing actor, while the public official who authorised, contracted, or supervised them remains the operationally responsible party.

Chapters 3–4

Taxonomy and threshold

Every case is assigned one primary sector, may carry up to three secondary sector tags, must carry one Governance Outcome Classification, and must carry at least one Pattern Detection Tag. These tags prevent thematic drift and make later sector-level, institution-level, and pattern-level analysis possible.

Primary sectorsCore structural fields
Elections, judiciary, finance, procurement, infrastructure, education, health, security, rights, policy decisions, institutional conduct, environmentGovernance Outcome Classification: Institutional Success, Institutional Resilience, Institutional Failure, Institutional Misconduct, Institutional Breakdown. Pattern tags include Procurement Failure, Regulatory Capture, Judicial Delay, Political Interference, Bureaucratic Breakdown, Accountability Evasion, and Positive Precedent.

A case qualifies if it is documentable, civic in consequence, and meets two of four triggers: democratic relevance, institutional relevance, public impact, or precedent value. The threshold is intentionally designed to allow procurement failures, child deaths, court judgments, reforms, and symbolic institutional conduct to coexist without the Archive becoming noise.

Chapters 7–9

Evidence and confidence

The naming standard cuts both ways. A person may only be named as a responsible party where primary source evidence places them in the described role at the described time. Naming is mandatory where that evidence exists and prohibited where it does not.

ClassEvidence standard
Class ACourt judgments, tribunal rulings, audit reports, budgets, appropriation acts, gazettes, procurement records, Hansard, sealed official correspondence, FOI responses, formally published EFCC or ICPC findings, INEC publications, CBN and regulatory notices
Class BGovernment press releases, annual reports, formally tabled parliamentary committee reports, regulatory agency notices
Class CInvestigative journalism with cited underlying documents, peer-reviewed research, credible international reports with documented methodology
Class DCredible reporting without named primary sources, civil society reports with partial documentation, ongoing legal filings
Class ENamed testimony, community reports with corroborating detail, verified photographic or video evidence

To publish a case as documented, the Archive requires at minimum one independent Class A source plus at least one supporting source of Class B or higher. Every factual claim must be independently corroborated. Every Research File must also record the Best Missing Document: what source should exist, what legal or procedural requirement mandates it, and what steps were taken to obtain it.

Confidence RatingsEvery published case carries a visible confidence rating: Verified, Strong, Moderate, Contested, or Under Review. The rating is not decorative. It is a public statement of evidential depth and limitation.

Chapters 10–11, 18–19

Research and review

Cases enter through four channels: internal research, journalist submission, CSO submission, and public tips after the first 100 entries are published. Every intake is assessed within 10 working days against the threshold rule and given an initial CIS score before research begins.

The methodology requires a 12-pilot launch protocol rather than an immediate bulk launch. Twelve pilot cases, one from each sector, are used to test the full methodology, audit gaps, calibrate scoring consistency, and certify launch readiness before public release.

StageRequirement
Research FileEvery case must have a permanent file containing the proposal form, source pack, evidence log, Best Missing Document, FOI log, right-of-reply record, review records, legal review where applicable, publication authorisation, traceability log, and all updates
TraceabilityEvery editorial action is named, dated, and attributable to a specific person; no case may publish with a traceability field blank
Right of replyMandatory, not courteous; named parties receive a formal notice and documented response window
Four-stage reviewResearch Director review, blind review, Editorial Lead review, and legal or Board review where required
Chapters 12–13, 20–22

Template and lifecycle

Every published case carries mandatory structural fields including case ID, version number, title, date range, sector, governance outcome classification, pattern detection tags, geography, CIS score, confidence rating, entry status, sensitivity tier, institutions involved, non-state actors, the event, the institutional chain, the evidence, the outcome, the governance question, citizen action, right of reply log, and change log.

LayerPurpose
Layer 1 — The RecordPure factual narrative of what happened, in plain language, without accountability mapping or analysis
Layer 2 — The Institutional MapThe accountability chain, the evidence pack, and the documented outcome
Layer 3 — The Governance InsightThe accountability question and the diagnostic note: what the case reveals about the system

Entries are never deleted. They move through documented states: Active, Contested, Under Review, Resolved, Superseded, and Closed. Every version remains publicly accessible. The change log records every update with date, reason, and authorising editor.

Document preservation is part of the publication standard itself. Every primary source must exist in three forms before publication: the original location, a downloaded copy on the Archive’s secure server, and an archived copy submitted to the Internet Archive.

Chapters 23–27

Outputs and governance

The methodology is explicit about bias, selection pressure, and the evidential limits of what an archive can document. Its answer is not denial. It is coverage targets, documentation availability notes, non-response tracking, quarterly sampling audits, and an Annual Bias Audit conducted by an Advisory Board member who did not serve as an editor during the period under review.

OutputFunction
The ArchiveThe structured, searchable public database of cases with version history and confidence ratings
The FOI TrackerA standalone public database of every FOI request filed by the Archive
Annual State of Accountability ReportSector, institution, geography, and bias analysis built from the case base
Institution-Level Accountability IndexA later-phase output released only when the Archive has sufficient depth
Sector ReportsThematic outputs on areas such as elections, judiciary, procurement, and education

Editorial authority is constitutionally separated from institutional governance. The Editorial Lead has final publication authority for Tier 1 and Tier 2 entries. The full Editorial Board has publication authority for Tier 3 entries. No governance body may direct or attempt to influence editorial decisions.
